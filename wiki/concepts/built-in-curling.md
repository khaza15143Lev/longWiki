# Built-In Curling

The permanent or semi-permanent curvature locked into a concrete pavement slab during construction due to temperature and moisture differentials between the top and bottom surfaces.

## Mechanism

Built-in curling develops when concrete is placed and cured under non-uniform conditions:
- **Summer/Daytime Paving**: Creates positive built-in curling (slab edges curl upward), which increases top-surface tensile stresses under subsequent traffic loading
- **Nonlinear Temperature Gradients**: Temperature profiles through the slab thickness are nonlinear, increasing top-slab tensile stresses by ~80% compared to linear gradient assumptions
- **Moisture Differential**: Differential drying between the top (exposed) and bottom (subbase contact) surfaces creates permanent moisture-induced curling

## Mitigation

- Late-afternoon paving significantly reduces positive built-in curling risk
- Controlled curing (fogging, wet burlap) reduces moisture differentials
- Wider slabs are more susceptible to built-in curling due to longer distance from edge to center

## Evidence

- [[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]] quantified positive built-in curling from summer paving in JPCP
- [[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]] demonstrated late-afternoon paving reduces early-age longitudinal cracking
- [[dere-asgari-sotelino-archer-2006-skewed-jpcp]] showed nonlinear gradients increase top-slab stresses 80% over linear models
