# Dowel Bars

Round steel bars placed across transverse joints in jointed concrete pavements to provide load transfer while allowing slab movement (expansion/contraction).

## Standard Practice

Per [[tayabji-trr-1062]]:
- Diameter: 1/8 of slab thickness
- Spacing: 12 inches center-to-center
- Length: 18 inches
- Most widely used load transfer device in modern practice

## Misalignment Types

Five categories identified by [[tayabji-trr-1062]] (Fig. 1):
1. **Horizontal translation** — Lateral shift parallel to joint
2. **Longitudinal translation** — Shift along dowel axis
3. **Vertical translation** — Up/down shift
4. **Vertical skew** — Rotation in vertical plane (causes spalling)
5. **Horizontal skew** — Rotation in horizontal plane (causes spalling + cracking)

## Effects of Misalignment

- Locked joints that restrain slab movement
- Transverse cracking, corner breaks, spalling around dowels
- Reduced load transfer effectiveness once spalling occurs
- Restraint stresses that may contribute to midslab cracking ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], Table 1)

## Tolerance Standards

- Historical FHWA: 1/4 in. per 18-in. length (removed in 1980 Advisory TS140.18)
- State agencies vary: Illinois (1/8 in. per 12 in.), Georgia (3/8 in. per foot)
- Laboratory evidence suggests misalignment ≤ 1 in. per 18 in. produces low pullout loads and no spalling at 0.25 in. joint opening ([[tayabji-trr-1062]])

## Placement Methods

- **Basket assemblies** — Preset dowel cages placed before paving; require rigid basket, proper fastening to subbase
- **Mechanical implantation** — Dowels inserted into plastic concrete during paving; higher noncompliance rates observed in field studies
