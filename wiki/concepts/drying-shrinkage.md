# Drying Shrinkage

Volume reduction in concrete caused by moisture loss during curing and service life, generating tensile stresses that can initiate cracking.

## Mechanism

Differential drying between the exposed top surface and the subbase-restricted bottom surface creates a moisture gradient. The top surface contracts more than the bottom, causing slab curling and tensile stresses at the surface that can exceed the concrete's early-age tensile strength.

## Key Findings

- [[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]] found strains at slab tops increased significantly after 2 months while bottoms remained constant, confirming differential drying shrinkage as the primary cause of top-down cracking
- [[mateos-harvey-2021-early-age-premature-cracking]] identified improper curing practices that accelerate surface moisture loss as a major contributor to early-age cracking
- Longer slabs experience greater cumulative shrinkage stresses, explaining why 5.8m slabs cracked while 4.0m slabs remained intact in FSHCC test sections

## Mitigation

- Controlled curing (wet burlap, fogging, curing compounds) reduces surface moisture loss
- Shorter joint spacing reduces cumulative shrinkage stress between joints
- Late-afternoon paving allows concrete to set during cooler nighttime hours, reducing differential drying rates
