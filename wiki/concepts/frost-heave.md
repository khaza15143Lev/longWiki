# Frost Heave

Upward displacement of soil and overlying pavement caused by ice lens formation during freezing of frost-susceptible subgrade soils. A primary mechanism of [[longitudinal-cracking]] in cold climates.

## Mechanism

Three frost-related scenarios identified by [[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]]:

**Case 1 — Center Heaving** (most common): Snow cleared from road center but piled at edges. Center freezes faster, creating upward heave. Tie bars stiffen central slab portion, lifting it as a unit. Longitudinal rupture occurs beyond tie bar ends.

**Case 2 — Edge Heaving**: Edges freeze and heave before center. Tie bar stiffening causes longitudinal rupture.

**Case 3 — Edge Sinking**: Non-uniform thaw in spring + heavy vehicles on softened subgrade produces cracking.

## Soil Susceptibility

Frost-susceptible soils (per [[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]]):
- Colby silt-loam (Wisconsin): fine-grained, dense, plastic; colloidal clay 19-33%; PI 5.5-20.9
- Discolored water issuing from center joints in spring confirms frost action

## Mitigation

- Elevated "high line" profile with fill from wide ditches reduces frost penetration
- Mesh reinforcement reduces cracking severity (95% reduction in longitudinal cracking on SH-13)
- Joint design should permit bending at joint during warping action

## Relationship to Expansive Soils

Both frost heave and [[expansive-soils]] involve moisture-related volume change, but frost heave is seasonal (freeze-thaw cycle) while expansive soil movement is moisture-driven year-round. [[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]] identified expansive soils (PI=30-39) as cause of differential settlement cracking in Colorado — a different climate mechanism but similar distress pattern.
