# Longitudinal Cracking Case Studies

Inventory of all documented field projects and case studies exhibiting longitudinal cracking in the wiki knowledge base, organized chronologically.

## 1935 — Wisconsin Highway 13 (Clark & Taylor Counties)

**Source:** [[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]]
**Location:** State Highway 13, Clark and Taylor Counties, north-central Wisconsin
**Soil:** Colby silt-loam (fine-grained, dense, plastic, frost-heave susceptible)
**Mechanism:** Frost heave — three scenarios: center heaving, edge heaving, edge sinking
**Findings:**
- Cracking confined exclusively to Colby silt-loam soil boundary; ceased abruptly at soil type change
- Tie bar spacing paradox: closer spacing (2 ft) produced MORE cracking than wider spacing (4 ft) because tie bars stiffen the central portion
- Reinforcement dramatically reduced cracking: 49-lb mesh reduced longitudinal cracking from 88 ft/100 ft to 4.2 ft/100 ft (95% reduction); 1/2-in. bar mat reduced from 40.5 ft/100 ft to 5.2 ft/100 ft (87%)
- "High line" profile (elevated roadbed with wide ditches) reduced frost heave effects; SH-29 east of Abbotsford showed little cracking
- Projects surveyed: 147-Re, 147-S, 332-B, 31

## 1982 — Georgia DOT Projects

**Source:** [[tayabji-trr-1062]]
**Location:** Multiple Georgia DOT projects (261 joints evaluated)
**Findings:**
- 60–83% of implanted dowels out of specification tolerance
- Zero dowel-related distress observed at any evaluated joint
- All joints started "working" within days of construction
- Significance: demonstrated that specification noncompliance does not necessarily correlate with distress

## 1986 — Texas Saw-Cut Depth Projects (near Houston)

**Source:** [[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]] (Saraf & McCullough)
**Location:** Two projects near Houston, Texas
**Mechanism:** Probabilistic saw-cut depth validation

| Project | Aggregate | Predicted Confinement | Observed Confinement | Required Depth for 90% |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Project 1 | River gravel | 67% | 69% | 0.44D |
| Project 2 | Limestone | 91% | 99% | 0.39D |

**Significance:** Lower standard deviation in limestone mix strength enabled better crack control at shallower saw-cut depths

## 1987–1999 — Colorado Region 1 (Three Sites)

**Source:** [[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]]

### I-70 Near Agate (MP 338.3–348.15)
- **Built:** 1998–1999; **Distresses found:** August 2001
- **Two crack types:** (1) straight cracks from improper vibration; (2) wandering cracks with differential settlement from expansive soils (PI=30–39)
- **Remediation:** reference pins, core drilling at crack ends, steel rebar cross-stitching

### US-287 at Kit Carson
- **Built:** 1998; **Length:** 9 miles; **Slabs:** 14-ft wide, 9 in. thick
- **Cracking rate:** Only 0.30% of slabs cracked
- **Causes:** Both shallow saw-cuts AND vibrator trails contributed
- **Split tensile strength testing:** inconclusive

### I-70 at Deer Trail (MP 326–327)
- **Built:** 1987; **Slabs:** 12-ft wide, 10.25 in. thick
- **Saw-cuts:** measured 2–2.25 in. (35% shallower than D/3 requirement)
- **Mix:** Lower cement content (565 lb total, including 110 lb fly ash)

**Significance:** CDOT adopted two new specifications: saw-cut depth monitoring every 528 ft and vibrator frequency monitoring devices

## 1995–2000 — Palmdale FSHCC Test Sections (California)

**Source:** [[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]]
**Location:** Palmdale, California
**Sponsor:** Caltrans, UC Berkeley Pavement Research Center
**Mechanism:** Top-down cracking from environmental loading (no traffic)

**Key findings:**
- 37 of 74 slabs cracked under environmental influences before any traffic loading
- 5.8 m (19 ft) slabs cracked; 4.0 m (13 ft) slabs remained intact
- After 2 months, strains at slab tops increased significantly while bottoms remained constant
- JDMDs showed significant corner curling with as much as 2.5 mm daily movement
- Corner loading creates stresses ~40% higher than midslab edge loading
- Permanent upward curl at corners due to differential drying shrinkage

**Significance:** First definitive documentation of top-down cracking mechanism; disproved bottom-up fatigue model for longitudinal cracking

## 2000s — Louisiana Statewide Survey

**Source:** [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]]
**Location:** 67.5 miles across 43 projects, Louisiana (avg age 20.3 years)
**Sponsor:** Louisiana DOTD, FHWA (LTRC Project 124P)

**Key findings:**
- Longitudinal cracking consistently higher than transverse cracking regardless of pavement age
- IRI dramatically increased for roads >30 years
- Widening from 13 ft to 15 ft: transverse cracking reduced 84%, longitudinal cracking increased 199% (174→520 ft/mi)
- Tied concrete shoulders vs. curb: longitudinal cracking increased 374% (174→825 ft/mi)
- One project: 86% of slabs with longitudinal cracks, 4,163 ft/mi
- ABAQUS FEA (288 cases): 127 showed longitudinal cracking potential (LCR > 1.0)
- Developed first empirical prediction model: log10(LC) = b0 + b1·TI + b2·L + b3·W + b4·(LW/D²) + b5·Shoulder + b6·Mr + b7·h·∛EBS (R² = 0.5423)

**Significance:** First study to demonstrate longitudinal cracking exceeds transverse cracking in JPCP; developed empirical prediction model; challenged AASHTO Pavement ME design assumptions

## 2000s — Indiana SR-49 (Valparaiso)

**Source:** [[dere-asgari-sotelino-archer-2006-skewed-jpcp]]
**Location:** State Route 49, Valparaiso, Indiana
**Sponsor:** Indiana DOT (SPR-2643)
**Mechanism:** Skewed joints (15°) with nonlinear temperature gradients

**Key findings:**
- Despite 15° skewed joints, ~70% of observed cracks oriented perpendicular to lane direction
- Nonlinear temperature gradients increased tensile stresses by 80% at slab top
- PCA fatigue equation predicted ~4-year design life, matching observed 3–4 year premature failure
- 5.49 m (18 ft) and 5.79 m (19 ft) slabs more susceptible than 3.66 m (12 ft) and 3.96 m (13 ft)
- Shrinkage strains (equivalent -5°C top surface change) increased tensile stress by 20–23%

## 2000s — US-78 Marshall County (Mississippi)

**Source:** [[uddin-et-al-trr-1482]]
**Location:** US Highway 78, Marshall County, Mississippi
**Sponsor:** Mississippi DOT, FHWA

**Key findings:**
- Nine test sections evaluated with GPR, thermographic survey, coring, FWD, Dynaftect
- Backcalculated moduli: uncracked concrete = 3,494 ksi; cracked concrete = 1,850 ksi (47% reduction)
- ABAQUS dynamic deflections matched measured FWD deflections for uncracked, cracked concrete, and cracked concrete+base conditions

## 2010s — Texas Longitudinal Joint Repair Projects

**Source:** [[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]]
**Location:** Seven Texas projects
**Sponsor:** Texas DOT (TxDOT)

| Project | Pavement | Distress | Repair | Outcome | Cost |
|---------|----------|----------|--------|---------|------|
| IH35 Waco | CRCP | 2-in. separation | Slot stitching (2010) | Excellent after 2 yrs heavy traffic | $118,560 (741 bars) |
| US75 | JCP | Faulted slabs, weak base | Slot stitching + polyurethane lift | Good; FDR failed <1 yr | — |
| LP410/IH45 Houston | CRCP | Stabilized base | Stapling (Material B) | Good after >8 years | — |
| US290 Houston | CRCP, 10-in. | — | Stapling (improper filler) | Filler cracking/spalling/debonding | — |
| US59 Atlanta | JCP | No dowels, no base | Cross stitching + foam level | Failed within 2 years | — |
| SH289 | JCP, 9-in. | Lime-treated base | Cross stitching | Good after 6 yrs; uncertain effectiveness | — |

**Key findings:**
- Slot stitching most cost-effective: ultimate shear load 12.0 kips vs. 6.7 kips for stapling; half the cost of full-depth repair
- Cross stitching failed on US59 within 2 years due to faulted slabs and poor LTE
- Weak base/subgrade (DCP: base modulus 15–30 ksi vs. expected >50 ksi; subgrade CBR = 5) caused recurring FDR failures

## 2010s — Korean Expressway

**Source:** [[seo-kim-2013-dowel-longitudinal-cracking]]
**Location:** Korean expressway (Korea Expressway Corporation)
**Sponsor:** National Research Foundation of Korea (NRF 2010-0007816)

**Key findings:**
- 6 of 400 joints (1.5%) showed longitudinal cracking
- Cracks observed before pavement opened to traffic; propagated through cement (not aggregates)
- Cores confirmed cracks at dowel locations with dowels installed far above mid-depth
- Some cores showed horizontal delamination at dowel depth

## 2010s — Iowa Widened JPCP Sites

**Sources:** [[kaya-et-al-2019-widened-jpcp-longitudinal-cracking]], [[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]]
**Location:** Multiple Iowa widened JPCP sites
**Sponsor:** Iowa DOT

**Key findings:**
- All longitudinal cracks are top-down, starting 0.6–1.2 m (2–4 ft) from widened slab edges
- Most cracks initiate from transverse joints
- Four-axle truck with center of axle loads near transverse edges identified as critical loading scenario
- Tied PCC shoulders outperformed HMA and granular shoulders
- HMA shoulder LTE of only 10% between shoulder and slab bases; similar behavior to no shoulder
- Site constructed in late afternoon (concrete set during nighttime) only slightly exhibited longitudinal cracks
- Pavements with skewed joints prone to longitudinal cracks under all loading scenarios
- Traffic volume correlates with cracking severity
- Sites with higher curling/warping measurements exhibited significantly more cracks

## 2010s — California PaveM Database

**Source:** [[saboori-et-al-2021-pavement-me-calibration]]
**Location:** ~4600 lane-miles across 446 lane replacement projects (1947–2017), California
**Sponsor:** Caltrans (PPRC SPE 3.49)

**Key findings:**
- JPCP with widened concrete shoulders (WRF) do not perform well; predicted performance similar to flexible shoulders
- Lean concrete base worst performer among JPCP base types
- Dowels don't impact cracking performance (neither presence nor absence)
- Thicker/shorter slabs perform much better than thinner/longer slabs
- Untied flexible shoulders and no shoulders show more cracking than tied concrete shoulders
- Worst performance in Inland Valley, High Mountain, South Mountain regions
- National model overpredicts by ~13%; bias is 19% for long slab pattern, 3.3% for short slab pattern
- California: ~17% of total individual slab replacements cracked by age 5

## 2010s — LTPP SPS-2 Sections

**Source:** [[xu-cebon-2017-lttp-cracking-analysis]]
**Location:** LTPP SPS-2 sections nationwide
**Sponsor:** FHWA (FHWA-HRT-16-073), University of Cambridge

**Key findings:**
- Two longitudinal crack patterns: (1) single long crack ~1 m from pavement edge; (2) short crack near centerline
- Most longitudinal cracks initiate from slab transverse edges
- Most transverse cracks initiate from slab longitudinal edge close to shoulder
- Dry climatic zones: cracks occur earlier and are more severe than wet zones
- Arkansas (wet zone): edge pumping occurred as result of cracking rather than vice versa
- Severity sensitive to slab thickness and base type; slab width and strength have less clear effect

## 2010s — Illinois Stabilized Subbase Survey

**Source:** [[desantis-roesler-2022-stabilized-support-layers]]
**Location:** Multiple Illinois sections
**Sponsor:** Illinois DOT (R27-193), FHWA

**Key findings:**
- HMA stabilized subbases performed well across tested sections
- PCC subbase erosion in poor drainage locations (underpasses, sag vertical curves)
- Bonded concrete inlay over existing composite pavement (HMA over PCC) was erosion-susceptible
- CAM II mixes with lower cement contents more susceptible to erosion
- Longitudinal cracking from subbase erosion spans multiple slabs

## 2020s — Wisconsin/Illinois/Minnesota Dowel Spalling Sites

**Source:** [[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]]
**Location:** Six sites in WI, IL, MN
**Sponsor:** MnDOT Office of Research & Innovation, ARA Inc.

| Site | Location | Findings |
|------|----------|----------|
| Site 1 | WI: I-39 | Large spalls (≥1 ft) at transverse joints within 10–15 years |
| Site 2 | WI: US 53 | Large spalls at transverse joints |
| Site 3 | IL: I-94 | Illinois Tollway: spalls progress from small to large within 2 years; equally likely on approach or leave side |
| Site 4 | MN: TH 610 | Large spalls at transverse joints within 10–15 years |

**Key findings:**
- MIRA scanning revealed subsurface delamination on joints with no visible surface distress
- Cracks extend several feet from joints, through multiple dowel bars, sometimes full transverse joint length
- Cores further from joints show cracks passing through aggregate (propagation after concrete gained strength); cores closer to joints show cracks around aggregate (early-age cracking)
- ACPA trigger/variant framework: two triggers + three variants = "definite" cracking risk

## 2020s — California Early-Age Cracking

**Source:** [[mateos-harvey-2021-early-age-premature-cracking]]
**Location:** California statewide
**Sponsor:** Caltrans (65A0628), PPRC Project 4.74

**Key findings:**
- ~17% of total individual slab replacements cracked by age 5
- Statistical model predicts 50% cracking after 10 years for 0.7 ft undoweled slabs on heavily trafficked roads
- Worst 20% reach same condition in <5 years
- Widened slab debate unresolved: SPS-2 and Louisiana show increased cracking; Colorado and Wisconsin attribute to construction issues
