# Longitudinal Cracking

Cracking in portland cement concrete pavements (PCCP) that runs parallel to the direction of traffic, typically along or near the pavement centerline or shoulder joint. One of the most common premature distresses in concrete pavements.

## Causes

Two groups identified by [[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]]:

**Group 1: Cracking with Differential Settlement**
- High swell potential subgrade soils (high plasticity index, lower R-value)
- Poor subgrade compaction
- Manifests as wandering cracks that meander between lane center and wheel paths

**Group 2: Cracking without Differential Settlement**
- Shallow [[saw-cut-depth]] at shoulder joints (less than D/3 for 12-ft slabs, less than 0.4D for 14-ft slabs)
- [[vibrator-trails]] from malfunctioning or improperly adjusted paver vibrators
- Cracks appear straight and at consistent distance from pavement edge

## Evidence

- Cores from non-working shoulder joints showed saw-cuts 35-50% shallower than specification ([[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]], p. 10)
- Petrographic analysis confirmed lower air content in vibrator trail cores vs. adjacent concrete ([[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]], pp. 7-8, Appendix C)
- 14-ft slab design explicitly ruled out as a contributing factor ([[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]], p. 11)

## Remediation Methods

- Cross-stitching with epoxy-coated steel rebar ([[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]], p. 6)
- Core drilling at crack ends to arrest propagation
- Crack sealing to prevent water infiltration
- Reference pin installation for monitoring crack width and differential settlement
