# Longitudinal Joint Separation

Separation or opening at the longitudinal construction joint between adjacent concrete pavement lanes or slabs. Distinct from [[longitudinal-cracking]] (cracking within a slab) though both produce linear distress parallel to traffic.

## Causes

Per [[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]]:
- Tie bar rupture from dynamic loading by heavy truck traffic
- Tie bar corrosion reducing cross-section and bond strength
- Improper tie bar placement or absence of tie bar installation
- Pumping and voids beneath joint reducing support
- Weak base/subgrade (base modulus <50 ksi, CBR <5)

## Indicators

- Load Transfer Efficiency (LTE) < 40% from FWD testing
- Visible lane separation (up to 2 inches observed)
- Faulting at longitudinal joint
- Pumping evidence, voids detected by GPR

## Repair Methods

Three methods evaluated by [[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]]:

| Method | Best For | Shear Capacity | Relative Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slot stitching | Wide separations, faulted slabs | 12.0 kips | 1x (baseline) |
| Stapling | Tight separations, no faulting | 6.7 kips | 2x |
| Cross stitching | Tight cracks only | N/A | — |

## Performance

- Slot stitching with Class P concrete filler: most cost-effective, performs well under heavy traffic
- Stapling with proper elastomeric filler: good performance >8 years; quality control critical
- Cross stitching: limited to tight cracks; ineffective on faulted slabs or poor LTE conditions
