# Slab Geometry

The length, width, and thickness dimensions of a concrete pavement slab, which determine the distribution of critical tensile stresses under traffic loading and thus influence whether cracking occurs in the transverse or longitudinal direction.

## Longitudinal Cracking Ratio (LCR)

Defined by [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]]:
- LCR = σCx / σCy (transverse critical tensile stress / longitudinal critical tensile stress)
- LCR < 0.8: slab unlikely to crack longitudinally
- LCR 0.8-1.0: cracks may initiate in either direction
- LCR 1.0-1.2: longitudinal cracks very likely
- LCR > 1.2: slab will definitely crack longitudinally

## Geometry Effects

Per [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]]:
- **Wider slabs** reduce length/width ratio, shifting critical stress location from longitudinal edge to transverse joint
- **Tied concrete shoulders** further change geometry, creating critical tensile stress at right wheel path
- **Thinner slabs** develop more longitudinal cracking than thicker slabs
- **Longer slabs** have larger potential for transverse rather than longitudinal cracking
- Width-to-thickness ratio of 1.2-1.5 recommended to minimize cracking (Wisconsin study)

## Design Implications

- AASHTO Pavement ME considers mid-slab transverse cracking as primary JPCP distress; longitudinal cracking NOT predicted
- Widened slabs and tied shoulders effectively reduce transverse cracking but increase longitudinal cracking — a design tradeoff requiring mechanistic-empirical model
- NCHRP 1-51 investigating slab/underlying layer interaction for MEPDG
