# Soil Voiding

Formation of voids beneath concrete slabs caused by slab curl depressurizing fine-grained soils in dry zones, leading to loss of support and cracking.

## Mechanism

When a slab curls upward at edges due to temperature or moisture differentials, it creates negative pressure (suction) in the soil beneath the lifted area. In fine-grained soils and dry climates, this depressurization causes soil consolidation and void formation. The resulting loss of support leads to cracking independent of traffic loads.

## Key Findings

- [[xu-cebon-2017-lttp-cracking-analysis]] proposed the mechanism where slab curl depressurizes fine-grained soils in dry zones, validated through LTPP SPS-2 data
- [[kaya-et-al-2019-widened-jpcp-longitudinal-cracking]] observed voiding beneath widened slabs in Iowa, particularly at transverse joints where top-down cracks initiate

## Design Implications

Fine-grained subgrades in dry climates are particularly susceptible. Traditional support models assume full contact; soil voiding requires coupled hygro-thermo-mechanical analysis.
