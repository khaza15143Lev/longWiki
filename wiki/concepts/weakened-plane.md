# Weakened Plane

A deliberately created zone of reduced cross-section in a concrete pavement slab that controls where cracking occurs. Saw-cut joints are the primary method of creating weakened planes in PCCP.

## Design Principle

Concrete shrinks and experiences thermal stresses during curing and service. A weakened plane ensures that when internal stresses exceed concrete tensile strength, cracking occurs at the joint rather than randomly within the slab.

## Key Requirements

Per [[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]]:
- Saw-cut must be deep enough to create effective weakened plane: D/3 for 12-ft slabs, 0.4D for 14-ft slabs
- Shallow cuts fail to create weakened plane, causing uncontrolled longitudinal cracking to relieve stresses (p. v)
- Cores from "non-working" shoulder joints (where joint did not crack through) confirmed saw-cut deficiency (Fig. 9, p. 10)

## Failure Modes

When weakened plane fails to form:
- Cracks develop elsewhere in the slab to relieve internal stresses
- Cracking may follow vibrator trails (secondary weakened plane from over-consolidation)
- Random longitudinal cracking replaces controlled joint cracking
