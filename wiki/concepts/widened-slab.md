# Widened Slab

Concrete pavement slabs wider than the standard 12-ft lane width, typically 13-15 ft, often used with tied concrete shoulders to improve structural capacity and reduce transverse cracking.

## Tradeoff

Per [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]]:
- **Benefit**: Widening 13→15 ft reduced transverse cracking 84% (40.9→6.7 ft/mi)
- **Cost**: Longitudinal cracking increased 199% (174→520 ft/mi)
- **Mechanism**: Changed length/width ratio shifts critical fatigue damage from longitudinal edge to transverse joint, creating longitudinal crack potential

## Historical Context

- [[ardani-et-al-2003-colorado]] found 14-ft slab design NOT a contributing factor to premature construction-related longitudinal cracking in Colorado (validated LTPP SPS-2)
- [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]] found widened slabs DO increase longitudinal cracking under traffic fatigue in Louisiana
- Resolution: Colorado studied early-age construction defects (saw-cut depth, vibrators); Louisiana studied long-term fatigue. Both can be true in different contexts.

## Design Guidance

- [[xiao-wu-2018-louisiana-longitudinal-cracking]] recommends width-to-thickness ratio of 1.2-1.5 (Wisconsin study reference)
- Slab geometry (length, width, thickness) should be carefully designed to minimize longitudinal cracking potential
- AASHTO Pavement ME does NOT currently predict longitudinal cracking for JPCP
