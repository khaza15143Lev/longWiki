# Evaluation of Premature PCCP Longitudinal Cracking in Colorado

**Report No.** CDOT-DTD-R-2003-1  
**Published** January 2003  
**Sponsor** Colorado Department of Transportation (CDOT), FHWA

## Overview

This Colorado Department of Transportation report documents the investigation of premature longitudinal cracking in portland cement concrete pavements (PCCP) across three sites in Region 1: I-70 near Agate, US-287 at Kit Carson, and I-70 at Deer Trail. The study identifies two primary causes and results in two new CDOT specifications aimed at preventing recurrence.

## Key Claims

1. **Shallow saw-cut depth is the primary cause** — Shoulder joint saw-cuts less than 1/3 of slab thickness (D/3 for 12-ft slabs, 0.4D for 14-ft slabs) fail to create a weakened plane, causing random longitudinal cracking to relieve internal stresses (p. v, p. 11).

2. **Malfunctioning vibrators create vibrator trails** — Vibrators set too high or prolonged vibration at low paver speed over-consolidates concrete, reducing air content and forming weakened planes along vibrator paths (p. v, pp. 7-8).

3. **14-ft slab design is not a contributing factor** — The study explicitly cleared the wider slab design, validating CDOT's 1996 adoption based on LTPP SPS-2 research (p. v, p. 11).

4. **Expansive subgrade soils contribute to differential settlement** — Soils with high plasticity index (PI=30-39) and poor compaction cause longitudinal cracking with differential settlement, though these were isolated cases in the study (p. 4, p. 6, p. 11).

5. **Core evidence confirms air content reduction** — Petrographic analysis by CTL showed cores from vibrator trails had consistently lower air content than cores taken 18 inches away in the center of the lane (p. 7, Appendix C).

## Notable Quotes

- "Examination of cores from several locations on US-287 and I-70 revealed longitudinal joint saw-cuts that were less than 1/3 of the thickness of pavement, i.e., not deep enough to instigate weakened plane cracking. As a result, longitudinal cracks developed to relieve the internal stresses in the concrete." (p. v)
- "Vibrator frequency set too high and/or prolonged vibration at low paver speed was determined to be the main factor that caused longitudinal cracking along the vibrator trails." (p. v)
- "This study found that the 14-foot-wide slab design was not among the causes of longitudinal cracking. CDOT adopted the 14-foot slab design in 1996 based on the results of the LTPP SPS-2 experiment, and a supplemental study conducted by Dr. Michael Darter of ERES." (p. 11)

## Field Investigation Sites

### I-70 Near Agate (MP 338.3-348.15)
- Built 1998-1999, distresses found Aug 2001
- Two crack types: (1) straight cracks from improper vibration, (2) wandering cracks with differential settlement from expansive soils (PI=30-39)
- Remediation: reference pins, core drilling at crack ends, steel rebar cross-stitching

### US-287 at Kit Carson
- Built 1998, 9 miles, 14-ft wide slabs, 9 inches thick
- Only 0.30% of slabs cracked
- Both shallow saw-cuts AND vibrator trails contributed
- Split tensile strength testing inconclusive (Table 1, p. 9)

### I-70 at Deer Trail (MP 326-327)
- Built 1987, 12-ft wide slabs, 10.25 inches deep
- Saw-cuts measured 2-2.25 inches (35% shallower than D/3)
- Lower cement content mix (565 lb. total, including 110 lb. fly ash)
