# Repairs of Longitudinal Joint Separations and Their Performances

**Journal** Construction and Building Materials, Vol. 54, pp. 496–503  
**Sponsor** Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT)

## Overview

Evaluates three repair methods for longitudinal joint separations and slab faulting in CRCP and JCP across seven Texas field projects: slot stitching, stapling, and cross stitching. Provides laboratory comparison, field case studies, cost analysis, and performance recommendations.

## Key Claims

1. **Tie bar failure is root cause** — Absence, corrosion, or rupturing of tie bars at longitudinal construction joints causes larger slab deflections, pumping, and ultimately lane separation, cracking, and faulting ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], pp. 496-497).

2. **Low LTE indicates separation risk** — Load Transfer Efficiency (LTE) values below 40% consistently found at longitudinal joints with separation; determined from FWD testing ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], p. 497).

3. **Slot stitching most cost-effective** — Restores load transfer and horizontal anchorage using deformed bars installed in saw-cut slots at mid-depth. Ultimate shear load: 12.0 kips vs. 6.7 kips for stapling. IH35 project: $160/bar (741 bars), half the cost of full-depth repair ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], pp. 498-499).

4. **Stapling performance depends on filler material** — U-shaped bars at max 3-in. depth (vs. 6-in. for slot stitching); filled with low-modulus elastomeric concrete. Houston district: some projects performing well >8 years. Material B (elastomeric) outperformed Material C (cracking, spalling, debonding observed). Stapling costs 2x more than slot stitching ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], pp. 500-501).

5. **Cross stitching limited to tight cracks** — Alternating diagonal holes with #6 deformed bars and epoxy. Failed on US59 (faulted slabs, poor LTE) within 2 years. Effective on SH289 (tight cracks, strong base support, good aggregate interlock), but effectiveness uncertain — unstitched cracks showed similar performance ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], pp. 501-502).

6. **Weak base/subgrade causes recurring FDR failures** — US75: DCP showed base modulus 15-30 ksi vs. expected >50 ksi; subgrade CBR = 5, modulus = 7 ksi. Full-depth repairs failed in <1 year because weak foundation not replaced ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], p. 499).

7. **Class P concrete filler recommended** — Least expansive slot filler material; performed well without longitudinal movement after repairs on IH35 and US75. Requirements: 7-day flexural ≥570 psi or compressive ≥3500 psi; 28-day flexural ≥680 psi or compressive ≥4400 psi ([[chen-2014-longitudinal-joint-repairs]], pp. 498-499).

## Notable Quotes

- "Slot stitching has been used successfully to restore load transfer along longitudinal joint and provide horizontal anchorage to prevent further lane separation." (p. 499)
- "The cost would be double if full depth repair was used instead of slot stitching to repair the wide longitudinal joint separations." (p. 496)
- "Cross stitching maybe used to repair cracks/separations that are fairly tight. For wider cracks/joint separations and/or faulted slabs, cross stitching is not suitable." (p. 502)

## Case Studies Summary

| Project | Pavement | Repair | Outcome | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IH35 Waco | CRCP, 2-in. separation | Slot stitching (2010) | Excellent after 2 yrs heavy traffic | $118,560 (741 bars) |
| US75 | JCP, faulted slabs, weak base | Slot stitching + polyurethane lift | Good; FDR had failed <1 yr | — |
| LP410/IH45 Houston | CRCP, stabilized base | Stapling (Material B) | Good after >8 years | — |
| US290 Houston | CRCP, 10-in. | Stapling (improper filler) | Filler cracking/spalling/debonding | — |
| US59 Atlanta | JCP, no dowels, no base | Cross stitching + foam level | Failed within 2 years | — |
| SH289 | JCP, 9-in., lime-treated | Cross stitching | Good condition after 6 yrs, but uncertain effectiveness | — |
