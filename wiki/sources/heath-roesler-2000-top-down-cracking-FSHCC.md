# Top-Down Cracking of Rigid Pavements Constructed with Fast-Setting Hydraulic Cement Concrete

**Journal** Transportation Research Record 1712, Paper No. 00-0974  
**Sponsor** California Department of Transportation (Caltrans), UC Berkeley Pavement Research Center

## Overview

Investigates top-down cracking of FSHCC test sections in Palmdale, California under environmental loading (no traffic). Uses ILLI-SLAB (ILSL2) FEA and field instrumentation to demonstrate that differential drying shrinkage combined with nonlinear negative temperature gradients causes top-down cracking, with longer slabs cracking while shorter slabs remain intact.

## Key Claims

1. **Top-down cracking from environmental loading** — 37 of 74 slabs cracked under environmental influences before any traffic loading. Cores confirmed cracking began at top of slabs and propagated downward ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 13-14, 421-423).

2. **Differential drying shrinkage primary cause** — After 2 months, strains at tops of slabs increased significantly while bottoms remained constant. Increase due to drying shrinkage, not thermal contraction. Differential strains through slab thickness resulted in slab bending stresses exceeding concrete strength ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 427-434).

3. **Longer slabs crack, shorter don't** — Maximum stress for longer (5.8m) slabs was ~13% greater than flexural strength (5.14 MPa). Maximum stress in shorter (4m) slabs was 23% lower than longer slabs and less than flexural strength. Explains why longer slabs cracked, shorter remained largely uncracked ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 400-406).

4. **Nighttime worst-case gradients** — Highest concrete tensile stresses developed at top of slab under extreme negative temperature gradients (during night). Both shrinkage differential and nonlinear nighttime temperature gradients increase tensile stresses at top ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 386-392).

5. **Corner loading critical** — Load plus environmental stress analysis showed critical failure location at corner of slab, not midslab edge. Stresses for midslab edge loading with positive temperature gradient and no shrinkage gradient were ~40% lower than corner loading case ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 407-417, 448-451).

6. **Permanent upward curl** — JDMDs showed significant corner curling with as much as 2.5mm daily movement. Corner deflection data suggested slab corners permanently curled upward due to differential drying shrinkage, resulting in unsupported corner condition at all times ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 435-443).

7. **FSHCC not inherently high-shrinkage** — Laboratory testing showed some CsA cements could have lower shrinkage than Type II cements. FSHCC does not necessarily indicate high shrinkage differentials ([[heath-roesler-2000-top-down-cracking-FSHCC]], pp. 455-468).
