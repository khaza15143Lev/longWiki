# Longitudinal Cracking of Concrete Pavements on State Highway 13 in Clark and Taylor Counties, Wisconsin

**Publication** Highway Research Board Proceedings, pp. 157-169  
**Sponsor** University of Wisconsin, Wisconsin Highway Commission

## Overview

Classic 1935 field study documenting excessive longitudinal cracking in concrete pavements built on Colby silt-loam soil in north-central Wisconsin. Establishes frost heave as the primary mechanism and evaluates the effects of tie bar spacing, reinforcement, and roadbed elevation on crack development.

## Key Claims

1. **Cracking confined to specific soil type** — Excessive longitudinal cracking found exclusively on Colby silt-loam soil; cracking ceased abruptly when road passed to other soil types. Soil: exceedingly fine-grained, dense, plastic, subject to excessive frost heaving ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], pp. 157-159).

2. **Frost heave is the mechanism** — Three frost-related scenarios identified:
   - **Case 1 (center heaving)**: Snow removed from center, edges insulated; faster freezing at center causes upward heave. Stiffened central portion (from tie bars) lifts as unit, causing rupture beyond tie bar ends.
   - **Case 2 (edge heaving)**: Edges heave before center; tie bar stiffening causes longitudinal rupture.
   - **Case 3 (edge sinking)**: Non-uniform thaw + heavy vehicles on soft subgrade produce cracking ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], pp. 166-167, Fig. 6).

3. **Tie bars stiffen central portion** — Tie bars lacing two slabs together prevent lateral movement but also stiffen the center. Cracks form 2.5-4 ft from centerline, approximately at ends of tie bars. Closer tie bar spacing (2 ft) produced MORE cracking than wider spacing (4 ft) ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], pp. 166-168).

4. **Reforcement dramatically reduces cracking**:
   - 49-lb mesh: longitudinal cracking reduced from 88 ft/100 ft to 4.2 ft/100 ft (95% reduction); transverse cracking reduced 44%
   - 1/2-in. bar mat: longitudinal cracking reduced from 40.5 ft/100 ft to 5.2 ft/100 ft (87% reduction); transverse cracking reduced 27%
   - All cracks in reinforced sections were extremely narrow/tight ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], Tables II-IV, VI).

5. **"High line" profile reduces cracking** — Elevated roadbed with fill material from wide ditches at either side reduced frost heave effects. SH-29 east of Abbotsford (high profile) showed little cracking ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], pp. 167-168).

6. **Pavement cross-sections show center peak** — Accurate surveys confirmed pavement heaved upward at centerline in frost-affected areas, consistent with Case 1 theory ([[janda-1935-wisconsin-highway-13]], Fig. 7).

## Notable Quotes

- "The Colby silt-loam soil is an exceedingly fine-grained soil, dense and plastic... conducive to frost heaving, and this action is probably the real reason for the longitudinal cracking of the concrete pavements in this area." (p. 166)
- "The longitudinal joint should be so designed as to maintain the central edges of the slab at the same elevation yet permit bending at the joint when a warping action is present." (p. 169)
