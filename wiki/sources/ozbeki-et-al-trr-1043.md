# Evaluation Methodology for Jointed Concrete Pavements

**Publication** Transportation Research Record 1043, pp. 1–7  
**Sponsor** Pennsylvania Department of Transportation, FHWA

## Overview

Presents an objective methodology using the JSLAB finite element program to evaluate transverse joint structural adequacy through nondestructive testing. Establishes limiting thresholds for modulus of subgrade reaction (k) and modulus of dowel-concrete interaction (G) to guide rehabilitation decisions.

## Key Claims

1. **Two critical parameters** — Modulus of subgrade reaction (k) and modulus of dowel-concrete interaction (G) have the most significant effect on pavement deflections and stresses; other variables (dowel size, dowel modulus, joint width, concrete modulus) have minor impact ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], pp. 5–6).

2. **Limiting thresholds established** — k < 200 pci indicates poor subgrade; G < 200,000 pci indicates poor joint system. These thresholds separate subsealing needs from load transfer rehabilitation ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], p. 7).

3. **JSLAB validated against Westergaard** — Finite element solutions showed good agreement with Westergaard's exact equations for interior, edge, and corner loading cases ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], pp. 2–3, Fig. 1).

4. **Evaluation charts developed** — Figures 10 and 11 enable practitioners to back-calculate k and G from NDT measurements of corner deflection and joint efficiency ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], p. 7).

5. **Rehabilitation decision guidance** — Loss of subgrade support (low k) requires subsealing; loss of dowel-concrete interaction (low G) requires load transfer reestablishment. Wrong scheme wastes money ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], p. 7).

### Paper 3: Westergaard Solutions Reconsidered (Ioannides et al.)

6. **Westergaard equations often misquoted** — Reexamination using finite element method found several equations ascribed to Westergaard in literature are erroneous; original edge stress equation from 1926 is incorrect; 1948 equation should be used instead ([[ozbeki-et-al-trr-1043]], p. 13, abstract only).

## Notable Quotes

- "Most of the pavement rehabilitation programs now under way were undertaken on the basis of subjective engineering judgment. Typically an engineer will 'walk' a project to determine the type of repairs or rehabilitation needed." (p. 1)

- "The ability to distinguish between the loss of subgrade support and the loss of dowel-concrete interaction is important in the selection of rehabilitation treatments." (p. 7)
