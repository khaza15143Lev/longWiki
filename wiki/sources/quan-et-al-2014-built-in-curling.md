# Inclusion of Built-In Curling Temperature Profile in Curling-Stress Determination for Rigid Pavement

**Journal** Journal of Transportation Engineering 141(4), 06014003  
**Sponsor** Natural Science Foundation of Heilongjiang Province, China

## Overview

Develops approach incorporating nonlinearity of setting temperature gradient into curling stress determination for rigid pavement. Validates analysis with LTPP performance data comparing 47 summer-constructed vs. 43 cooler-season-constructed test sections.

## Key Claims

1. **Summer daytime paving worst** — Paving activities in daytime in hot summer lead to maximum positive built-in curling in rigid pavement, which increases both level and frequency of tensile stress on top of PCC slab ([[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]], pp. 525-528).

2. **Nonlinear temperature profile matters** — Current AASHTO MEPDG simplifies built-in curling to temperature difference between top and bottom of slab. Actual nonlinear setting temperature profile means concrete cannot reach zero stress state ([[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]], pp. 33-36, 70-73).

3. **Positive built-in gradient shifts stress** — Positive setting gradient (locked into slab) shifts insitu temperature profile in negative direction, making it easier for concrete surface to experience tensile stress ([[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]], pp. 60-64).

4. **LTPP validation** — Curling stress analysis validated by performance monitoring data in LTPP database. Comparison of 47 summer vs. 43 cooler season test sections showed pavement performance corresponded with curling stress analysis ([[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]], pp. 16-18, 529-530).

5. **New method eliminates temperature distribution dependence** — New approach differentiates paving conditions in curling stress analysis without dependence on temperature distribution curve along slab depth ([[quan-et-al-2014-built-in-curling]], pp. 518-521).
