# Solutions to Mitigate Dowel/Tie-Bar Propagated Cracking Phase 1

**Report** NRRA202402, Minnesota Department of Transportation  
**Sponsor** MnDOT Office of Research & Innovation, ARA Inc.

## Overview

Comprehensive Phase 1 study evaluating cracking related to dowel bars and tie bars in JPCP through literature review and field investigations at six sites in Wisconsin, Illinois, and Minnesota. Identifies delamination cracking at dowel bar depth as primary mechanism, with cracks initiating during early age and propagating under combined environmental and traffic loading.

## Key Claims

1. **Delamination initiates at dowel depth** — Cracks start around dowel bar depth near transverse joints as horizontal delamination cracks, then migrate upward to surface, eventually causing spalling. MIRA scans and coring confirm cracks extend several feet from joints even when no surface distress visible ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 504-505, 69-85).

2. **Early-age microcracking mechanism** — Steel bars (5-10× stiffer than early-age concrete) restrain concrete movement during hydration. Restraint stresses exceed low early-age tensile strength, causing microscopic damage. Microcracks coalesce over time under repeated thermal/moisture/traffic loading into visible delamination ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 480-481, 540-541).

3. **Cracks propagate through aggregate** — Cores further from joints show cracks passing through aggregate (indicating propagation after concrete gained strength). Cores closer to joints show cracks around aggregate (indicating early-age cracking when concrete was weak) ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 505, 85).

4. **Delamination visible on apparently intact joints** — MIRA scanning revealed subsurface delamination on joints with no visible surface distress. Cracks extend through multiple dowel bars, sometimes full transverse joint length ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 60-69).

5. **Spalls progress rapidly** — Time history from Illinois Tollway shows spalls progress from small to large within 2 years. Spalling equally likely on approach or leave side of joint ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 57-59).

6. **Six cracking mechanisms identified**:
   - Restraint cracking (temperature drop, hot-weather paving, high evaporation)
   - Subsidence/settlement cracking (high slump, low cover, large bar size)
   - Plastic shrinkage cracking
   - Misalignment-induced cracking (locked joints)
   - Chloride-induced delamination/corrosion
   - Tie bar over-reinforcing and edge restraint from tied shoulders ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 6-21)

7. **ACPA trigger/variant framework** — Early restraint cracking requires triggers (≥25°F temperature drop, >90°F paving, >0.1 lb/ft² evaporation) combined with variants (high friction base, late sawing, high cement factor, shrinkage-susceptible mix). Two triggers + three variants = "definite" cracking risk ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 7-9).

8. **Field sites show common pattern** — Six sites (WI: I-39, US 53; IL: I-94; MN: TH 610) all exhibited large spalls (≥1 ft) at transverse joints within 10-15 years. Spalls distinguishable from durability-related spalling by size, depth, and lack of hairline cracking ([[rao-abdualla-sufian-2024-dowel-tie-bar-cracking]], pp. 45-55).

## Notable Quotes

- "During this very early life, in effect, there is a race between concrete strength development and build-up of stresses from various sources of restraints and from early loads." (p. 540)

- "The results of the trenching operations suggested that the delamination action was caused by the combined action of multiple dowel bars (acting in sync) rather than that of one or two dowel bars." (p. 505)
