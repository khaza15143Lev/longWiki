# Controlling Longitudinal Cracking in Concrete Pavements

**Publication** Transportation Research Record 1043, pp. 8–13  
**Sponsor** Pennsylvania Department of Transportation, FHWA  
**Institution** University of Texas at Austin

## Overview

Develops a probabilistic model to estimate the depth of saw cut needed to control longitudinal cracking within the groove in wide concrete pavements. Uses lognormal distributions of concrete tensile strength and pavement thickness to predict crack confinement probability. Validates the model against field observations from two Texas projects near Houston.

## Key Claims

1. **Probabilistic crack confinement model** — Cracking occurs along the saw-cut (x-x) when the tensile strength ratio R = T1/T2 ≤ 1.0, where T1 is tensile strength at the saw-cut and T2 is tensile strength away from it. The model uses lognormal distributions of concrete tensile strength and pavement thickness to estimate the probability P[R ≤ 1.0] that cracks will be confined within the groove ([[saraf-mccullough-1986-sawcut-depth-probabilistic]], pp. 8–10).

2. **Aggregate type determines required saw-cut depth** — Two projects near Houston, Texas:
   - **River gravel mix**: required **0.44D** saw-cut depth for 90% crack confinement
   - **Limestone aggregate mix**: required only **0.39D** for 90% confinement
   The limestone mix had lower standard deviation in tensile strength, enabling better crack control at shallower cuts ([[saraf-mccullough-1986-sawcut-depth-probabilistic]], pp. 10–12, Fig. 3).

3. **Field verification matches predictions**:
   - **Project 1** (river gravel, TX-288 inside I-610): predicted 67% vs. observed 69% cracking along saw-cut joint
   - **Project 2** (limestone, TX-288 outside I-610): predicted 91% vs. observed 99%
   Both showed reasonable match between model and field observations ([[saraf-mccullough-1986-sawcut-depth-probabilistic]], pp. 10–11, Table 1).

4. **31% of river gravel cracks escaped the groove** — On Project 1, 31% of cracks developed away from the saw cut, compared to only 1% on the limestone project. This highlights the role of material variability in crack control ([[saraf-mccullough-1986-sawcut-depth-probabilistic]], pp. 10).

5. **Reducing variability allows shallower cuts** — Sensitivity analysis showed that halving or quartering standard deviations of concrete strength substantially increases crack confinement at the same depth ratio, potentially saving construction costs. Uniformity of concrete mix strength (represented by standard deviation) is a critical construction quality variable ([[saraf-mccullough-1986-sawcut-depth-probabilistic]], pp. 12–13).

## Notable Quotes

- "A lower value of standard deviation obtained for concrete mix using lime rock aggregate in the mix was responsible for confining more cracks within the saw cut compared with the mix using river gravel aggregate." (p. 8)

- "A sensitivity analysis of the model indicated that substantial reduction in saw-cut depth can be achieved if the variability of concrete strength during construction can be reduced." (p. 8)
