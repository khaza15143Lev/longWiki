# Dowel Placement Tolerances for Concrete Pavements

**Publication** Transportation Research Record 1062  
**Sponsor** FHWA, Construction Technology Laboratories (PCA)

## Overview

Comprehensive investigation into allowable levels of dowel bar misalignment at concrete pavement joints. Combines theoretical analysis attempts, laboratory pullout testing on slab specimens, and review of field studies from multiple state DOTs to establish placement tolerance criteria.

## Key Claims

1. **Five types of dowel misalignment** — Horizontal translation, longitudinal translation, vertical translation, vertical skew, and horizontal skew. Only vertical skew and horizontal skew cause spalling; horizontal/vertical skew also causes cracking and reduces load transfer ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], Fig. 1).

2. **Pullout loads low for misalignment < 1 in.** — Laboratory tests on 8-in. and 10-in. slabs showed pullout loads remain relatively low for misalignment ≤ 1 in. per 18-in. dowel length at joint opening of 0.25 in. No spalling observed below this threshold ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], pp. 52-54, Tables 7-8).

3. **Pullout loads increase sharply above 1 in.** — For 10-in. slabs at 7 days: 0 in. misalignment = 750 lb; 1 in. horizontal = 2,000 lb; 2 in. horizontal = 4,000 lb; 4 in. horizontal = 5,500 lb. Vertical misalignment produces higher loads than horizontal ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], Table 8).

4. **Analytical modeling infeasible** — Three-dimensional modeling of dowel-concrete slippage and misalignment geometry too complex for available FE programs (SAP4, BMINES). Laboratory testing was necessary ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], pp. 50-51).

5. **Field noncompliance widespread but no distress observed** — Georgia DOT study: 60-83% of implanted dowels out of specification tolerance, yet no dowel-related distress found at any evaluated joint. All joints started "working" within days of construction ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], pp. 49-50, Tables 4-5).

6. **FHWA does not specify numeric limits** — Technical Advisory TS140.18 (1980) removed specific tolerance limits but cautioned that "close tolerances for dowel placement are extremely important for proper functioning of the slab and for long-term performance" ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], p. 47).

7. **Locked joints cause multiple distress types** — Excessive restraint from misaligned dowels may produce transverse cracking, corner breaks, and spalling at concrete face around dowel. Once spalling occurs, load transfer effectiveness decreases ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], p. 47).

8. **No revision to current tolerances recommended** — Due to limited laboratory data and insufficient field performance data, existing state agency tolerances (typically 1/4 in. per 18 in.) should remain, though data suggest greater tolerances may be acceptable ([[tayabji-trr-1062]], p. 54).

## Notable Quotes

- "Before December 1980, FHWA specified limits on dowel placement. However, the current FHWA Technical Advisory TS140.18 of December 15, 1980, on rigid pavement joints does not specify limits on misalignment but cautions that 'close tolerances for dowel placement are extremely important for proper functioning of the slab and for long-term performance.'" (p. 47)
- "Laboratory test results indicate that pullout loads are relatively low for dowel misalignment levels of less than 1 in. per 18-in. length of dowel bar and a maximum joint opening of 0.25 in." (p. 53)
- "It is clear from Table 5 that there is substantial noncompliance with the specification requirement for the projects with the implanted dowels. However, no dowel-related distress was found in any of the joints that were examined." (p. 50)

## Field Studies Reviewed

| Study | Method | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Indiana (Smith & Benham, 1938) | Lab pullout tests | 1/2 in. joint opening: errors up to 1.5 in. tolerated without spalling |
| Alabama (Segner & Cobb, 1967) | Lab tests, 10-in. slabs | 1-in. vertical misalignment: ~4,000 lb; 1-in. horizontal: ~2,000 lb |
| Pennsylvania (Nichols & Hoffman, 1980) | Pachometer + cores | 60% implanted, 40% basket dowels out of tolerance |
| Georgia DOT (1982) | Cores + metal detector, 261 joints | 60-83% implanted dowels out of spec; zero distress observed |
| Alabama (Beeson et al., 1981) | Visual + metal detector | No significant difference between implanted vs. preset dowels |
