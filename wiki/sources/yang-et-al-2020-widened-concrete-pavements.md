# Investigation of Longitudinal Cracking in Widened Concrete Pavements

**Journal** The Baltic Journal of Road and Bridge Engineering 15(1), pp. 211-231  
**Sponsor** Iowa Department of Transportation (TR-700), Iowa State University

## Overview

Field investigation and FEA of longitudinal cracking in widened JPCP using Terrestrial Laser Scanner for curling/warping measurements and concrete coring. Identifies combination of excessive traffic, high curling/warping, inadequate support, pavement ageing, and skewed joints as primary causes.

## Key Claims

1. **Top-down cracks 0.6-1.2m from edge** — Top-down longitudinal cracks observed in widened JPCP, mainly located within traffic lanes 0.60-1.20m (2-4ft) from slab edges, parallel to traffic direction ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1450-1453).

2. **Five causes of longitudinal cracking** — Combination of: (1) excessive loads, (2) high degrees of curling/warping, (3) inadequate subgrade support, (4) pavement ageing, (5) skewed joints ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1475-1477).

3. **Traffic volume correlates with cracking** — Sites subjected to higher truck traffic volume exhibited more significant number of longitudinal cracks ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1456-1457).

4. **Curling/warping measurements confirm** — Sites with higher degrees of curling and warping measurements exhibited significantly more longitudinal cracks than sites with lower degrees ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1458-1460).

5. **Tied PCC shoulders outperform** — Sites with tied PCC shoulders generally outperformed those with HMA and granular shoulders, even though tied PCC sites experienced relatively higher traffic volume ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1461-1464).

6. **Late-afternoon paving reduces cracking** — Site constructed in late afternoon, where concrete set during night-time and led to less built-in curling, only slightly exhibited longitudinal cracks ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1465-1467).

7. **Skewed joints prone to cracking** — FEA results indicate pavements with skewed joints are prone to longitudinal cracks compared to rectangular joints under all loading scenarios ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1468-1471).

8. **Recommendations** — At most 4.30m (14ft) for widened slab width; late-afternoon paving to avoid excessive upward built-in curling; avoid skewed joints and granular shoulders for widened JPCP with high truck traffic ([[yang-et-al-2020-widened-concrete-pavements]], pp. 1477-1482).
